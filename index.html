FIM Classification Analysis

Seunga Ha1 and Handeul Choi1
1Ghent University Global Campus

This project investigates how training choices shape model behavior in Flow Imaging Microscopy (FIM)-based protein particle classification. Rather than treating performance as a single score, we compare trends across experimental configurations and particle classes to identify what the model reliably uses as evidence.

By combining class-wise metrics with qualitative visualizations (e.g., confusion heatmaps), we highlight stable strengths, persistent failure modes, and augmentation-sensitive classes. These results guide practical decisions for improving robustness and provide interpretable insights into which particle attributes (shape, texture, scale, context) drive classification.

Model and Training

Task Overview

We train a deep learning classifier to predict ten protein particle categories (mAb1–mAb10) from FIM images. To address high inter-class similarity, the dataset is split into train/validation/test sets and trained with dynamic augmentations designed to promote generalizable feature learning.

Model selection is based on macro-averaged F1, precision, and recall, and is complemented by class-wise analysis. This combination ensures that the chosen configuration performs consistently across classes rather than being dominated by a small subset of easier categories.

Optimization

We evaluated ~15 experimental configurations by varying normalization, transformation pipelines, and training settings. Instead of optimizing for a single global metric, we prioritized configurations that improved class-wise stability and reduced systematic confusions between visually similar particles.

The best-performing setup showed consistently strong behavior across evaluation criteria despite the intrinsic similarity of protein particle morphology. Its advantage appears to come from exposing the model to controlled spatial diversity (e.g., variations in size and orientation), which encourages more transferable visual representations.

Key Observations

Across most configurations, we observe a consistent hierarchy in class difficulty: some particles remain stable and separable, while others show recurring confusions even when the pipeline changes. This indicates that difficulty is not uniformly distributed and that a subset of classes may require targeted strategies rather than generic tuning.

In particular, configurations that combine resizing-based spatial diversity with background/appearance controls tend to improve stability. These trends suggest the model benefits from learning invariant cues rather than overfitting to orientation- or scale-specific shortcuts.

Factors Influencing FIM Classification

Augmentation Sensitivity–Robustness

We profile robustness across ten mAb categories under seven controlled transformations. The Net Score summarizes stability: positive values indicate robustness (insensitivity), while negative values indicate sensitivity to specific perturbations.

The top robust classes (mAb1, mAb3, mAb5) maintain high recall even under perturbations, implying that the model extracts reliable cues for these particles. In contrast, sensitive classes show performance drops that reveal which visual factors (e.g., luminance, orientation, scale) the model depends on.

Particle Insights

Based on Net Scores and sensitivity patterns, we group particles by the type of visual evidence the model appears to rely on. This provides an interpretable mapping from model behavior to plausible particle attributes (shape, texture, scale/context, heterogeneity).

Conclusion

Our results show that robustness is strongly class-dependent in FIM protein particle classification. Particles with distinct morphological “fingerprints” remain stable across perturbations, while ambiguous or heterogeneous classes are vulnerable to changes in luminance, orientation, or scale.

This profiling framework helps identify which visual attributes the model uses—and where it fails—so future improvements can target specific confusions and design augmentations that strengthen invariances rather than shortcuts.
